Soil Degradation:


The deteriorating soil quality is becoming a challenge as the prospects of increasing food production for rising population is being jeopardized. There is increasing evidence to suggest that majority of Indian soils are getting sick and their production potentials are on the decline. Maintaining soils in a state of high productivity is essential for the functioning of life support system and socio-economic development of the agrarian economies. It is necessary to maintain soil health and quality without causing undesirable side effects like pollution, physical, chemical and biological degradation. The developments of suitable strategies to reverse the process of degradation, improve or ameliorate the soil quality are the key issues that watershed projects must address. The deeper understanding of physical degradation through soil erosion, chemical degradation through fertility loss, nutrientimbalances and contamination and biological degradation through loss of biological life through excessive use of harmful pesticides is essential to promote environment friendly production strategies.


The soil resource bulletin of Jammu Kashmir state provides basic information on soils, their characteristics, classification, extent distribution, problems and production potentials. The rock out crops, ice caps and glaciers constitute about 56.7% of the total 22.2 mha area and is not fit for agriculture. The soils of J&K belong to 4 orders, 9 suborders, 15 great groups, 27 sub-groups and 66 families. About 7 mha (31.6%) area is affected by various forms of degradation out of which 24.6% suffer from serious problem of water erosion causing loss of top soil and or terrain deformation, 6.1% is affected by mind erosion in Ladakh region and 0.9% by water logging and flooding in the valley. Only 11.7% area has stable terrain with no soil degradation problems as such. About 56% of the area affected by water erosion comes under high severity of soil degradation and 33% under medium severity class of soil degradation.

 

In case of wind erosion, 63% area comes under medium and 34% under high severity class. The physical deterioration is of medium severity in 81% area and low severity in 15% area. One may conclude that water erosion is the main degradation problem in which loss of topsoil is the most serious concern. The loss of topsoil is clearly indicated by several soil profiles. For example, in the Leh region, the A horizon is hardly of 5cm depth and B horizon is present only in two profiles out of seven taken on a 40km stretch of landscape. Only one profile taken on Indus river terraces has a profile of A&B horizons upto about 50cm depth. In case of greater and lesser Himalayas of Kashmir region, one hardly finds topsoil in A horizon on side/reposed slopes as exemplified by profiles at Chinamarg and BREN.


However, very deep soils are found on upper piedmont plains, karewas and terraced valley lands. Loamy skeletal soils with top 50 cm profile full with gravels are also found at places. Deep alluvium with well-defined layers of mature profiles are found in flatter valley areas in Budgam and Srinagar area. In case of Lesser and Greater Himalayas of Jammu region, over the lithology of shale, granite and sand stone medium to deep soil profiles are found only in valley areas of Chenab river (up to 150 cm depth), and valley lands of Kishtawar . However, on side and reposed slopes, the depth of A horizon is les than 20 cm, and B horizon is altogether missing and parent material is located very close to the soil surface. Such soils are most problematic as vegetation cover development is a real problem on shallow coarse loamy, calcareous, and skeletal soils.

 

Well developed deep soil profiles are found in alluvial plains of RS Pura (old flood plains) and along Tavi River (Recent Flood Plains) where fine and coarse loamy soils have been developed over alluvium and colluvium (transported and deposited soil mass) in Jammu region. In case of Shivalik Hills of Udhampur; up to 150 cm deep and well-developed soil profiles over sand stone are found where A horizon extends up to 50 cm soil depth and B horizon is also well developed. As one moves up to Chineni and Patnitop, the depth of A horizon goes on decreasing and B horizon is altogether missing. The western Himalayas of which J&K forms an important part lies in a climatic zone which experience south-western monsoons and 70 to 80% of the annual rainfall occurs in three months i.e. from mid June to end September. High intensity monsoon rains falling on sleep slopes devoid of adequate protective vegetation cover and composed of erodible soils create most conducive conditions for high run off and soil loss. The inappropriate cultivation along the slope, grazing by ever increasing number of livestock, excessive removal of trees for fuel fodder and timer has disturbed the ecological balance and lead to severity of floods, drought, land slides and flows, soil sinking, soil slips, siltation of reservoirs and low lying areas.

 

A report of National Bureau of Soil Survey and Land Use Planning, ICAR Nagpur (NBSS62) published in the year 2000 the highlights of seriousness of soil degradation problems in all the four regions except terraced valley lands and piedmonts of karewas. In such lands, the problems of soil erosion by water may be less serious but the problems of chemical and biological degradation caused due to excessive use of chemical (insecticides, pesticides, weedicides, fungicides) are getting more and more pronounced.

 

Fertility Status of Ladakh Soils: An extensive survey of 96 villages of Leh and 50 villages of Kargil districts of Ladakh region were carried out to determine the fertility status of soils. Soils were alkaline in certain pockets and saline particularly in fields located near Sindh River. Only 15% soil samples in Leh and 32% in Kargil were in high category of organic carbon due to heavy dozes of organic manures and night soil. Available nitrogen was generally low due to slow mineralization of organic carbon in cold climate and losses of nitrogen through leaching in light textured soils. The nutrient index of available phosphorus was low in Leh and medium in Kargil. In case of available potassium, 45% soil samples of Leh and 56% of Kargil were in high range and the remaining in the medium range. The micronutrients were found deficient in more than 60% of soil samples except for copper. Zinc was deficient in 48% soil samples. Soils of Kargil were superior in nutrient status as compared to the Leh soils.

 

Livestock related degradation concerns:


The almond and apple orchards of the Kashmir Valley support grass growth underneath their canopies that meets the forage needs of the livestock. The apple (scab) and almond plants suffer from variety of insect and pest attacks and hence need large number of sprays of insecticides and pesticides that fall on the grass growing underneath the fruit plants during the process. The consumption of such grass by the livestock can cause poisoning and health problems. Once it enters the food chain, these chemical substances cause toxicity in human beings as well. Some pesticides are systematic in nature, which adversely affect the population of friendly insects e.g. the pollinators like bees and butter flies.


Both overland flow of run off water and the pesticides contaminate sub-surface flow of ground water, which pollutes springs, streams and the water bodies. In addition to human and livestock health problems, inland fish is the next major casualty.Several livestock diseases particularly foot and mouth, rinder pest are common in alpine pastures. The diseased animals, when they migrate to lower areas of the hills, spread communicable diseases to the livestock of lower areas as well as wild animals as they commonly share the alpine pastures.Jammu and Kashmir is a big market of meat products because of the food habits of the people. As a result, livestock is brought to the slaughterhouses of the state from Punjab, Haryana and Rajasthan. Though quarantine facilities have been provided at Lakhanpur check post to ensure that no diseased animal enters the state but checks are not very rigorous.


Intensive grazing by sheep and goat is not good for forests but in silvi-pasture systems, it helps to keep the bushes under check as most bushes are heavily browsed. But due to militancy problem, the graziers do not visit several pastures and as a result the grasslands have gradually been infested with bushes.Oxytocin is a commonly used hormone, which is injected into milch animal to induce milk. Harmful effects of oxytocin in human beings have been reported; and therefore, the use of oxytocin is being discouraged by the Animal Husbandry (AH) Department. There are no proper slaughterhouses situated away from the populated areas. Dead animals emit foul smell. In absence of proper animal graveyards, carcasses are thrown in nalahs where they decay. Even when the poultry birds die due to various diseases, they are just being thrown away at isolated places and not buried properly.

 

 

Forest Degradation:


The actual forest area (satellite data by FSI) is 20,433 km2 though as per forest records it is 20,230 sq. km out of which 54% is dense and 46% open forest. As per FSI 1995 report, 9.13% accounts for the actual worthwhile forest area with a crown density above 40%. The coniferous forests are spread over 8,127 km2 and broad leaved over 12,055 km2. The area under forest is 50.97% in Kashmir, 45.89% in Jammu and 0.06% in Ladakh. Out of 101,387 sq. km geographical areas, an area of 20230 sq. km constitutes 19.95% that is much below the level of 66% prescribed for hill states in the National Forest Policy of 1988. The pasture and grazing lands within the demarcated forests are 2,655 and 1,933 km2 in Jammu and Kashmir, which constitute 57.9% and 42.1% respectively of the pasturelands. According to a 1998 report, nearly 99 sq km of forest cover has been damaged since 1991. Some 20 sq km of forest cover has changed to non-forest/scrub.

 

56.6% households meet 50-75% of their fodder needs from forest and about 97% households meet 80% of their timber needs from forest. An average family in Kashmir consumes 7 tons of fuel wood. The total out turn of timber in 1995 was 112,880 cum which was 205,582 in proceeding two years and fuel wood decreased to 5,450 cum as compared to the average plus 6,104 cum for the proceeding 10 years. According to one estimate, 56.6% households meet 50-75% of their fodder needs from forest and about 97% households meet 80% of their timber needs from forest. An average family in Kashmir consumes 7 tons of fuel wood. In six districts of Kashmir there are 134,149 ha of cultivable waste land, 128,943 ha of degraded permanent pasture and other grazing lands, 41,180 ha of follow land and 79,156 ha of degraded forest which shows that an area of 3.83 lac ha is available for sustainable development of forest resources in the Kashmir valley.


Large areas around Kashmir villages have been depleted of their forest cover and tree lines are receding to upper peaks. The majority of trees are heavily lopped and pollarded for fuel and fodder. One finds stacks of fuel wood in every house and entire economy appears to be wood based. There is rampant grazing in the forests of the Shiwalik region and areas close to villages have lost almost the entire tree cover and only unpalatable and uneconomical bushes have been left. Flooding, drought, siltation, water scarcity, out migration, drudgery and stress are the consequences of decline in forest cover. Major deforestation and habitat destruction is leading to displacement of communities to urban areas and multiplying the problems of slums. The existing natural forests and their wealth rich in biodiversity calls for urgent conservation based scientific protection and management. Doda presents a classical example where even the last surviving trees of Kuh scarcely dotted on barren steep land slopes are being axed. Due to deforestation leading to loose soils adjoining the river Chenab, the debris usually moves down the hills due to landslides causing blockage on the roads.

 

 

Forest degradation manifested in soil properties: In Kashmir Valley, the overall forest cover is diminishing but there are few areas that harbor dense forests. However the topography is uneven and slopes are moderate to steep where extensive topsoil erosion lends to presence of gravels, scanty vegetation and deterioration in site quality. In a study conducted by SKUAST Kashmir in 2005, site quality of two sites one with thick forest dominated by fir and spruce at high attitude and other at degraded areas was compared at five locations. The soil pH of the degraded site was more but available nitrogen phosphorus and potassium were consistently low at all the sites. Similarly, the bacterial and fungal population of degraded sites was 80 to 90 percent less than well-established forest sites. In case of Azotobacter, a free-living aerobic nitrogen fixer, the reduction in population was significant at degraded sites. In Gulmarg and Shopian sites, Azotobacter could not be recorded and at other sites the population was less by 50-66%. The population of P-solublizing bacteria in established forest stands of fir and spruce ranged from 2.7 to 23.5 x 104g-1 and at degraded site the same was in the range of 0.5  6.3 x 104g-1. The absence or low population of these organisms is indicative of low organic carbon, little biological activity and adverse conditions of the Rhizosphere which results is the loss of regeneration of preferred tree species.

 

Depletion of water resources:


Both rainfall and snow are the major sources of precipitation in the J&K state. While the Cold Arid Zone (Ladakh region) receives only 100-300 mm of annual rain but receives snow, which meets water requirements of the area. In the Kashmir valley, both rainfall (600-900 mm) and snow on peaks provide water needs. In the sub-topical region of Jammu Shiwaliks, 1000-1100 mm of rainfall is received annually but 80% occurs during three monsoon months and in rest of the nine months dry conditions are experienced. This is clearly indicated by the soil moisture balance data of Srinagar, Jammu and Leh. The monsoon rains are uncertain, erratic and highly variable over time and space. High intensity storms occur which cause severe soil erosion and flooding. The disappearance of protective vegetation cover on steeply sloping forest and pasturelands is causing sheet, rill, gully and stream bank erosion. As the major share of precious rainwater is lost as run off without recharging groundwater, the water resources like springs, wells, baulies are drying up. Due to climate change, the glaciers are receding at a very fast rate and snow-fed areas are facing increasing aridity. Due to heavy siltation, the area of lakes and water bodies is shrinking. The excessive use of chemical fertilizers and pesticides in vegetable and fruit crops is polluting the surface and sub-surface water sources through toxic elements injurious for human and livestock health. Use of banned pesticides also adds the woes related to toxicity in soils and water resources. The main rivers namely Jehlum, Chenab and Ravi are fast deteriorating in quality and quantity of water. In recent year, the incident of water borne diseases like cholera and Jaundice are on the increase mainly due to pollution of drinking water. There are no proper arrangements for solid waste management in urban and rural areas. Effluents are discharged in rivers and streams seriously affecting aquatic life including fish. The Dal Lake is getting filled with pollutants caused by tourists residing in Shikaras. Unplanned urbanization along lakes, rivers and water bodies is causing havoc to water resources. There is no proper water or land use policy of the State.

 

Threats to Wetlands of Jammu & Kashmir : Wetlands support highly valuable pools of biodiversity and genetic resources but unsustainable development is threatening the bio-wealth, causing species extinction and impact livelihoods of millions of people. The water system regulations, drainage of toxic discharge from farm lands, urban development and disposal of wastes in water bodies are the major causes of concern. Kashmir valley is replete with diverse types of fresh water bodies. Majority of these natural aquatic systems are shallowbasins of three types valley lakes of Kashmir (1580-1600m), Forest lakes of Pir Panjal Range (20002500 m), and glacial or high altitude lakes (3000 m). Valley lakes are drainage (Dal, Wular), semi drainage (Naranbaghsar, Khanpursar) and nondrainage (Trigamber) types and are mostly located in the flood plains of River Jhelum and Sind. In addition to diverse algal flora, most wetlands support emergents, rooted floating-leaf types, free floating forms and submerged ones of 117 species distributed in 69 generas. Kashmir aquatic systems including lake Wular-Ramsar site sustain good crop of valuable fishes, supporting livelihood of thousands of fishermen. Kashmir region accounts for more than 80% of the total fish production (1,600 tons in 1995-96) with Wular like contributing the maximum (60%) and River Jhelum (15%) and input from other lakes is very small 37 fish species have been recorded in Kashmir waters. Grass carp and silver carp were introduced at Trigam Fishfarm during 1981.


The diversity in Avifauna is quite considerable and varies seasonally. The lakes, wetlands and marshes provide ideal breeding grounds for many species including globally threatened ones. A total of 90 bird species were recorded. Importance of wetlands is demonstrated by the fact that a famine struck Kashmir during the end of 19th century and its impact was averted by the use of flour of Singhara (Trapa natans) a very common wetland plant. The wetlands of J&K state are under constant threat as their areas are shrinking, depth is decreasing due to deposition of eroded soil and waste materials disposed off in all natural drainage channels. The Dal Lake remains in news due to its shrinking area, disposal of effluents from Shikaras, heavy weed infestation and reduction in fish population. The major threats to Kashmir wet lands included the menace of increased siltation, Eutrophication due to run off from catchments laden with fertilizers and pesticides, encroachments from agriculture  land and willow plantation, receding open water areas as a result of expanding reed belts. Such dangers calls for integrated management practices for wetland conservation and preservation of their biological diversity.

 

 

Biodiversity Erosion :


Biodiversity is closely linked with the survival of human beings. The state of Jammu and Kashmir having four distinctly different biomes have rich faunal and floral biodiversity. Unfortunately, rapid erosion of biodiversity is recognized as one of serious environmental problem in the state. The rapid pace of deforestation, population explosion, habitat destruction, introduction of alien species, climate change, over exploitation of natural resources, faulty agricultural practices and industrial activities have seriously contributed to depletion of biodiversity. Large scale forest fires in the Shivalik region not only burn variety of grasses, bushes, and tree species but also large number insects, birds, animal and of reptiles are burnt alive and stunts the vegetative regeneration. The decomposers are eliminated along with the organic matter and leaf litter covering the soil surface. The vast spread invasion of alien weeds like lantana and parthenium is eliminating large number of legumes and palatable grasses.

 

Heavy soil erosion causes deterioration in habit quality and seriously impact floral diversity. Excessive grazing of preferred species also lead to their extinction from pasturelands. In the Ladakh region, several hardy bush species are being uprooted for fuel wood causing threats to their survival. The alpines pastures are being badly impacted not only because of climate change but also due to competitive grazing both by livestock and wildlife. Several wildlife species have gone extinct and some are on the verge of extinction. Large scale use of pesticides and chemical fertilizers in Horticulture have lead to the entry of toxic elements in streams, rivers and water bodies seriously impacting fish and other riverine aquatic life. The wetlands are getting silted due to heavy load of sediments discharged through run off from catchments areas.

 

 

Shivalik Region:

  • Extent of soil erosion, landslides, forest fires and bank cutting by torrents in the forest area located above the village are quite serious.

  • The soils are light in texture, poor in organic matter and there is shortage of FYM  major part of which is used as fuel. This is leading to low crop yields.

  • Wild life damage is quite severe. Stray cattle are usually let loose by poor farmers who cannot feed them which is deemed as another menace.

  • Weed infestations in forest area  Agiratum, Lantana, Parthenium, Bhang have spread to farmlands and have become a grave menace.

  • Common property resources are severely degraded. The common village lands are heavily grazed which is leading to decline in productivity.

  • Forage scarcity is felt throughout the year and fodder trees, are heavily lopped during winter.

  • There is an acute shortage of water. Most ponds dry up in summer and livestock goes to lower areas for watering.

Outer Himalayas:

  • Disappearance of protective vegetation cover under high biotic pressure from unstable steep slopes including loss of productivity and biodiversity.

  • Very severe soil erosion of all types though the gully erosion, land slides, land slips and road side erosion were more common leading to breaching of roads, debris flow to rivers and loss of soil and nutrients.

  • Loss of topsoil, lack of soil depth and resultant low water retention have limited the scope of introduction of superior, high value plant species. The extent of soil quality deterioration is so high that only few hardy zerophytic plant species were able to survive though they too are under a great threat for being lopped for use as fuel wood.

  • There was no land use policy. Even the smaller percentage of land with good productive potential is being put to unproductive purposes.

  • The undivided privately owned (Mushtarka Malka) pasturelands had almost lost everything, which supported plant life. Bare rock outcrops support only unpalatable hardy bushes that sprouts when some moisture is there but dry out quickly and are used as fuel wood in this biomass-starved economy. This was causing severe forage scarcity and revival of such lands was a big challenge.

  • Water scarcity and deterioration in water quality was a serious concern and the rural sanitation was poor. Heaps of cow dung, open defecation, filth is common.

  • Stone and sand querying and non-adoption of pollution control safeguards are rampant.

Kashmir Valley :

  • Forests have been recklessly ravaged during the last 15-18 years of militancy. People depend on forests for fuel wood, timber, grasses, and minor products. The wood is the mainstay of the economy. The trees are heavily pollarded for forage and fuel wood. Agroforestry is very popular on field bunds, buttresses and banks of nalahs.

  • Poor economic condition, lack of employment, backwardness, resource deprivation and natural resources degradation.

  • Chronic shortage of water though both rain and snow water flows through their land which cross into the river.

  • Stone quarry, poor quality of drinking water, discharge of sewage in water bodies, unhygienic conditions, use of pesticides in case of Apple at the risk of contamination, and siltation of water bodies with debris, filth and silt are very common problems.

  • Crop yields are very low and holdings are very small. The crop-growing season is from April to September in which upland maize and low land paddy are the principle crops. Maize has a very low yield and the Paddy meets only part of their food grain needs. Paddy straw is fed to the livestock throughout winter when it snows. There is no pesticides use in the villages. Fertilizer in the form of urea is added to paddy at a low rate of 20 to 30 kg/acre.

  • The forage scarcity is equally severe. The pasturelands have degraded and their productivity has dwindled. The milk yield on an average is 2 kg/day and most of the dung is used as farm manure.
     

Ladakh Region:

  • Wind erosion from sandy, barren, rainless landscape.

  • Removal of hardy bushes controlling wind erosion for fuel wood.

  • Receding glaciers due to climate change reducing water availability.

  • Wildlife extinction due to habitat deterioration. Wildlife includes Leopard, Lumbri, Dear, Spotted dear, antelope in eastern Leh.

  • Unplanned exploitation of wealth of medicinal/aromatic plants.

  • No solid waste management, no sewage system and very less tourist related sanitation and hygiene.

  • Pollution of water by various means.

  • Livestock related problems of communicable diseases brought by transhumant and transmitted to wildlife. Competition for grazing between wildlife and livestock is also becoming serious.
