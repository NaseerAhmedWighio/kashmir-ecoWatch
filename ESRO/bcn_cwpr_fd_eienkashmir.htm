Faunal Diversity of Jammu and Kashmir

 

The information on the fauna is available both in the form of natural history observations and status surveys .The analysis of the available data in three major faunal regions viz. cold desert, temperate and sub­, tropical reveals that much of the state's faunal diversity lies in the temperate regions (65.95 per cent) followed by cold desert (17.02 per cent) and sub-tropical (14.89 per cent).

 

The fauna of Jammu and Kashmir is diverse due to its unique geographical location. About 16 per cent of the Indian mammals, birds, reptiles and butterflies are represented in the state. Birds contribute much to the chordate diversity followed by mammals, reptiles, pisces and amphibians.

Mammals : The state is home to about 73 species of mammals beside several sub-species belonging to 54 genera, 21 families and 8 orders. Of the total mammals reported from the state, 20 are confined to the high altitude areas, 16 occur in the temperate areas and 3 in the sub-tropical areas. It is found that 21 species are commonly distributed in the transitional mountain system of temperate and cold desert, and 14 occur commonly in the temperate and sub-tropical areas

  • Carnivores : Carnivores represent 32 per cent of the total mammalian fauna in the state. Felids constitute the largest group comprising 34.78 percent followed by canids (17.39 percent), mustelids (30.34 percent), ursids (8.69 percent), and vivirids (8.69 percent). Among the carnivores leopard, snow leopard, Himalayan brown bear and Asiatic black bear, as well as wild dog are well known throughout their ranges in the state. Both the bears are much persecuted for illegal gall bladder and skin trades.Other smaller carnivores such as fox, martens, weasels are fairly common throughout their ranges. These animals are heavily persecuted and trapped for their fur. This has resulted in their extermination from many areas.

  • Ungulates : Of the 19 species and sub-species of the ungulates reported from the state, 13 have been listed as globally threatened, and receive complete legal protection through national legislation. Presence of large number of livestock owned by nomads in their prime habitat brings them into direct conflict by way of grazing competition. This also makes them vulnerable to epidemics such as 'boot fly parasitic disease' and rinderpest besides being subjected to local hunting. Consequently, many species have been wiped out from several areas in their ranges. Of the total ungulates reported from the state, 8 occur in the cold desert, 6 in the temperate forests and sub-tropical region of Kashmir , and 2 each in the sub-tropical areas and the plains of Jammu. The analysis also reveals that the bovidae family comprises much of the ungulate diversity followed by cervidae. These trends also suggest that most of the bovidae members are restricted to the high altitude areas while cervidae members to the temperate and sub-tropical regions.

Smaller Mammals : Smaller mammals include shrews, bats, squirrels, marmots, voles, rats, pikas, hare and porcupine, which together comprise 33 species, besides many sub-species. Rodents form the largest group representing 17 species followed by bats (8 species) and logomorphs (5 species). Of these, 11 species are restricted to high altitude areas, 12 to the temperate areas, 1 species viz. Indian porcupine to the sub-tropical areas, while Royle's vole is distributed in all the regions. The trends also suggest that 3 species each are commonly distributed in the transition area of temperate and cold desert as well as the transition area between temperate and sub-tropical region.  The data trend suggests that families such as Soricidae, Rhinolophidae and Zapodidae are mostly confined to the temperate areas, while Leporidae and Hystricidae to the cold desert and sub-tropical areas, respectively. Families such as Sciuridae, Cricetidae and Muridae are most widely distributed and occur throughout the state.

Birds : The bird diversity of the state varies seasonally and available data suggests that as many as 358 species belonging to 179 genera, 51 families under 16 orders. Most of the birds are migratory and arrive from the plains of the Indian sub-continent during summer mainly for breeding, but also by way of local migration. The lakes and marshes of the state provide ideal breeding ground for many species including globally threatened ones. About 225 species have been reported from Ladakh alone and as many as 262 from the temperate regions and 183 from the sub-tropical areas.  Arboreal birds represent the major part of the avian diversity followed by aquatic, raptors, waders and pheasants. The most dominant families in terms of species richness are Muscicapidae, Fringillidae, Anatidae, Accipitaridae and Charadiidae representing 22.70, 55.55, 57.50, 38.10 and 41.51 per cent of their total Indian representatives, respectively. These families together constitute about 48 per cent of the state's avifauna.  The available data suggests that families such as Podicipedidae, Phalacrocoracidae, Gruidae, Jacanidae. Rostratulidae, Ibidorhynchidae, Glareolidae, Pteroclididae, Psittacidae, Caprimulgidae, Coraciidae, Upupidae, Capitonidae, Troglodytidae, Certhidae, Nectarinidae and Zosteropidae are the least species rich families, represented by 1 or 2 species, and together comprising less than 6 per cent of the state's avifauna.  The available information also reveals that 72 species are resident in the state throughout the year, 237 are summer visitors and mainly come to breed, and 49 are winter visitors. The analysis of the data also reveals that maximum species find their ideal breeding habitats in the pine forests followed by mixed oak­pine forests, oak-deodar forests, semi deciduous forests, alpine meadows, rhododendron-fir forests, Juniper­birch forest and high altitude desertic areas with scrub. Thus, the avian diversity is found to be the greatest in the areas least affected by human interference.

 Amphibians/Reptiles : The state is a home to 14 species of amphibians belonging to 6 genera, 5 families and 1 order, and 68 species of reptiles belonging to 43 genera, 12 families and 2 orders. These include frogs, toads, agamid, turtles, lizards and snakes. The dominant families in terms of species richness include Colubridae (22 species), Gekonidae (13 species) and Agamidae (6 species). The rest of the families are represented by 1 or 2 species. Much of the herpetological fauna (about 78 per cent) is restricted to the sub-tropical areas of Jammu, and only less than 5 per cent to the temperate and high altitude areas. Although the cold climate of Kashmir and Ladakh region is not an ideal place for ectotherms like amphibians and reptiles, several species have adapted to these extreme cold conditions so well that they could be found at altitudes as high as 3500 m. As many as 53 species of reptiles occur in sub-tropical areas, 19 in temperate regions and 10 in the cold desert ecosystem.

Pisces : The available data suggests that 44 species of fishes belonging to 14 genera under 5 families occur in the state. It may be mentioned that trout were introduced many years ago in the state and have thrived well. The other species that probably pave been introduced into ponds and lakes of the state include silver carp (Cirrhinus cirrhosus), carp (Cirrhinus carpio), mirgal (Cirrhinus mrigala), grass carp (Cirrhinus sp.) kalbose (Labeo calbasu), rohu (Labeo rohita) and catla (Catla catla). These introductions are reported to have had an adverse impact on the native fish species.

Insects : The available data reveals that as many as 225 species of insects besides several sub-species belonging to 136 genera, 35 families, 4 orders occur in the state. These include butterflies, moths, wasps, ants, honeybees, flies etc. The analysis at a broad level suggests that Kashmir valley harbors most of the species (about 69 per cent). However, the analysis of the data on butterflies reveals that about 60 per cent species occur in the forests below 3000 m, 50 percent immediately above upto elevation of about 4200 m, 7 percent immediately above upto 5500 m, and less than 3 percent at extreme elevations above 5500 m.

 

 

Himalayas Faunal Biodiversity

 

The faunal studies in the Himalaya, dating back to the last quarter of the eighteenth century, have so far revealed over that besides insects, rich species diversity is noted in other invertebrate groups viz. Annelids, Spiders, Molluscs etc. The vertebrate faunal elements in the Himalaya provide a high degree of diversity at species level. Of the 372 mammalian species recorded in the country so far, as many as 241 species are recorded in the Himalaya; and of the 1,228 bird species as many as 528 species and subspecies occur in the region. Likewise 149 species of reptiles, 74 species of amphibians and 218 species of fishes have been documented from the Himalaya which amount to 35%, 36% and 17% of known species in the country respectively.

Of the total 622 endangered plants listed so far in the Red Data Books, 137 occur in the Himalayan region. Of the 137 species, 71 species are from the Eastern Himalaya, 56 species from the Western Himalaya, and ten species are common to both these regions.As many as 29 mammalian species listed under Schedule 1 of the Indian Wildlife Protection Act occur in the Himalaya. These include Slow Loris, Hoolock Gibbon, Brown Bear, Asiatic Black Bear, Spotted Linsang, Binturong, Marbled Cat, Goldern Cat, Himalayan Lynx, Clouded Leopard, Snow Leopard, Kiang, Pygmy Hog, Must Deer, Tibetan Gazelle, Tibetan Antelope, Hangul, Rhinoceros, Wild Buffalo, Takin, Himalayan Tahr, Ibex, Serow Markhor, Urial, Yak, Nyan and Hispid Hare.

The endangered avian species include Himalayan Bearded Baza, Tibetan Snowcock, Mountain Quail, Tibetan Blook Pheasant, Western Tragopan, Satyrs Tragopan, Blyths Tragopan, Temmincks Tragopan, Himalayan Monal Pheasant, Elwees Eared Pheasant, Cheoe Pheasant, Koklass Pheasant, Black necked Crane, Rufous neckled Hornbill etc. Most of the endangered avian species are recorded from Central or North West Himalaya.

In the case of fauna, several intertebrates are endemic to the Himalayan region. 15 of the earthworm species are endemic to North West Himalaya. Endemic vertebrate fauna include Snow Leopard, Slow Loris, Hoolock Gibbon, Tibetan Wolf, Tibetan Sand Fox, Himalayan Brown Bear, Red Panda, Spotted Linsang, Himalayan Lynx, Tibetan Wild Ass, Kashmir Stag and Himalayan Must deer etc.

Among the avian species, Himalayan Bearded Vulture, Mountain Quail and 10 species of Pheasants are endemic to the region. The number of species of Reptiles, Amphibia and freshwater fishes endemic to Himalaya are 29, 35 and 35 respectively.

 

India has 372 Mammals, 1175 Birds, 399 Reptiles, 181 Amphibians and 1500 Butterflies,  while as  Pakistan has 174 Mammals, 668 Birds, 177 Reptiles, 22 Amphibians, 998 Fishes (198 fresh ; 800 marine ) and over 5000 species of Insects. About 16 per cent of the Indian and 32 percent of Pakistan's mammals, birds, reptiles and butterflies are represented in the state of Jammu and Kashmir.
