Biodiversity is closely linked with the survival of human beings. The state of J&K, located in the far north is a mountainous area in the north-west Himalayas that shares international boundaries with Pakistan in the west, China in the north and Tibet in the north-east. Punjab and Himachal Pradesh are its neighboring states within the country. The three major territories, i.e. Jammu, Kashmir and Ladakh differ in terms of climate, physiography, ethos and culture. All the three regions experience different climatic patterns and distinctly different biomes, rich faunal and floral biodiversity. Within the Indian region, the area of Jammu & Kashmir is, with its dramatic geological history, broad latitudinal spread and immense altitudinal range, Kashmir Himalayas still spans a remarkable number of the world's broad ecological regions. This variety of habitats also supports a rich variety of different species which contributes to the overall biological diversity (or biodiversity) of the region. Kashmir has some of the worlds rarest animals and plants but these are now in danger of disappearing forever due to overuse and loss of natural habitat. The J&K mountains, foothills and plain house a zoogeographic diversity ranging from Oriental to Palearctic flora and fauna. Phytogeographically, the most complex. The flora has passed through various stages during the geomorphological evolution of this region. This region has been colonised at different times by humid tropical Malayan forms, tropical Africans forms, temperate and alpine north Asiatic - European forms. sclerophyllous Mediterranean forms, temperate East Asian ( Sino Japanese ) forms, and semi arid Central Asian forms. The rigorous environment has further acted upon this mosaic of geographical forms leading to the extinction of species, breaking up distribution ranges, or inducing genetic variations with or without speciation.

 

Unfortunately, rapid erosion of biodiversity is recognized as one of serious environmental problem in the state. There are several species of animals which have either been wiped out completely or are on the verge of local extinction due the rapid pace of deforestation, population explosion, habitat destruction, introduction of alien species, climate change, over exploitation of natural resources, faulty agricultural practices and industrial activities have seriously contributed to depletion of biodiversity. Large scale forest fires in the Shivalik region not only burn variety of grasses, bushes, and tree species but also large number insects, birds, animal and of reptiles are burnt alive and stunts the vegetative regeneration. The vast spread invasion of alien weeds like lantana and parthenium is eliminating large number of legumes and palatable grasses. Excessive grazing of preferred species also lead to their extinction from pasturelands. In the Ladakh region, several hardy bush species are being uprooted for fuel wood causing threats to their survival. The alpines pastures are being badly impacted not only because of climate change but also due to competitive grazing both by livestock and wildlife. Several wildlife species have gone extinct and some are on the verge of extinction. Large scale use of pesticides and chemical fertilizers in Horticulture have lead to the entry of toxic elements in streams, rivers and water bodies seriously impacting fish and other riverine aquatic life.

 

While people are without doubt a most valuable resource in Kashmir, uncontrolled population growth puts ever-increasing pressures on the states natural resource base. Misguided economic policies have widened inequalities and forced rural people and others to exploit biodiversity at rates that are no longer sustainable. As a result, processes such as deforestation, overgrazing, soil erosion, salinity and waterlogging have become major threats to the remaining biodiversity in Kashmir. It is now feared that state has the countrys second highest rate of deforestation. The continuing loss of this forest habitat with its associated fauna and flora will have serious implications for the nations other natural and agro-ecosystems. Just as more and more people may be part of the problem, they must also be part of the solutions. The key to protecting the biological heritage of Kashmir lies in the involvement of local people and in the support provided by competent institutions for the conservation and sustainable use of biodiversity.

 

The research community of Kashmir, under these increasing pressures, felt that their birthplace merited some "management in its management". Although the idea of creating a Biodiversity Conservation Network under the auspices of the eIEN Kashmir had been under consideration before the launch of the eIEN Kashmir itself, however it got impetus after the Project launch.

 

Initially the idea was to have a number of state and national level experts from different areas of specialization to provide advice to various biodiversity initiatives. The group was formally constituted and an announcement to this effect was made by the focal point on August 22, 2004. Now we are as many as 45 individuals on board. They included environmentalists, foresters, specialists from wildlife, livestock, agriculture, natural history, taxonomy, fisheries, tourism, zoology, biotechnology, protected area management, and development in general.

 

Objectives :

  • We have focus on the functional relationships and processes within Kashmir ecology.

  • We promote the fair and equitable access to the benefits derived from the functions of biological diversity and from the use of its components

  • We promote adoptive management practices

  • We carry out action programmes at the scale appropriate for the issue being addressed , with decentralizing to lowest level, as appropriate.

  • We ensure intersectoral co-operation.

The state of Kashmir may be called as the biomass state of India because it is known for export of its fruits and nuts, vegetables and their seeds, medicinal and aromatic herbs, honey, mushrooms and morels, saffron, condiments like caraway, silk and silk garments, wood carving and wood crafts, khutamband ceilings, papier mache, wicker work, sports goods, joinery items, etc,. Kashmir is also known for embroidery on silk, wool, and even on cotton garments, curtains and furnishings. All these materials and products are derived from biomass, much of which is from the rich biodiversity available in the state. 

 

Another traditional source of income has been tourism, which is also biomass related and biomass dependent: snowy mountains, fresh and clean air, crystal clear water, forests and greenery all round. 

 

Thus, Kashmir's trade and economy depends almost exclusively on biological raw materials. This character of the state  need not only to be maintained but also augmented.    

 

WHAT IS BIODIVERSITY ?

 

Biological diversity or "biodiversity" has been defined as: "the variability among living organisms from all sources including inter

alia, terrestrial, marine and other aquatic ecosystems and the ecological complexes of which they are part; this includes diversity within species, between species, and of ecosystems". (CBD 1992).

 

Genetic diversity) refers to variability in the functional units of heredity present in any material of plant, animal, microbial or other origin.

 

Species diversity is used to describe the variety of species - whether wild or domesticated ) within a geographical area. Estimates of the total number of species (defined as a population of organisms which are able to interbreed freely under natural conditions) range from 2 to 100 million, though less than 1.5 million have actually been described.

 

Ecosystem diversity refers to the enormous variety of plant, animal and micro-organism communities and ecological processes that make them function. In short, biodiversity refers to the variety of life on earth. This variety provides the building blocks to adapt to changing environmental conditions in the future.

 

WHY IS BIODIVERSITY IMPORTANT ?

Richness of species in an area indicates the total biodiversity of that particular area. However, it increases with the complexity of an ecosystem and vice versa. All species display genetic variation among individuals and populations. Genetic variation brings natural selection and adaptability to changes in the environment, which ultimately ensures species survival. Genetic diversity in domestic species and their wild relatives enables researchers to develop improved varieties of animals and plants for human needs. Diversity in wild plant species is major medicinal resource and 40% of the allopathic drugs were originally made from wild medicinal plants, and it is insurance for further food security. Biodiversity provides free of charge services worth hundreds of billions of rupees every year that are crucial to the well-being of Kashmiri society. These services include clean water, pure air, pollination, soil formation and protection, crop pest control, and the provision of foods, fuel, fibres and drugs. As elsewhere, these services are not widely recognized, nor are they properly valued in economic, or even social terms. Reduction in biodiversity (including local extinction of species) affects these ecosystem services. The sustainability of ecosystems depends to a large extent on the buffering capacity provided by having a rich and healthy diversity of genes, species and habitats. In that respect, biological diversity is like economic diversity in a city; it is essential for long term survival and a sound investment in the future.

Conservation of biodiversity also makes good environmental sense. The air we breathe, the water we drink and the soil that supports crop production are all products of the complex interactions that occur among various living organisms on earth. If these vital ecological services are damaged, so are the physical conditions maintained by the worlds species and ecosystems. Losing biodiversity is a bit like losing the life support systems that we, and other species, so desperately depend upon.

 

 

Conservation of biodiversity of Kashmir would actually ensure perpetual availability of biomass in the state which inturn would ensure the livelihood of the people of the state at large , especially the artisans. People orientated and biomass based micro and mega enterprises are the answer to the development of the state.

 

 The information on biodiversity of the state is scattered and rather incomplete. Biodiversity Conservation Network is facilitating development of computer based information management system at a single node , which would feed into decision support system.
